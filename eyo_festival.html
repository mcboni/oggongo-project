The Eyo Festival

By Titi Akintoye

photo credit: Travelstart Nigeria

Lagos is known for the Eyo festival, a very popular festival which is believed to have its origin in the secret societies of Lagos as it was held in the very distant past to escort the soul of a departed monarch or highly esteemed chief of the king’s court. The white clad Eyo masquerade represents the spirit of the dead (as all masquerades do). It is also celebrated to usher in a new king. The festival is also believed to be a dedication to the god of the sea- Olokun.

There is no customarily defined time for holding the festival but when it holds, it is usually preceded by a weeklong series of activities which culminates in a procession of men clothed in white flowing robes with their faces covered with see through woven material with everyone of them carrying a staff and wearing hats of different colours called “aga”.

Photo Credit:https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:EYO_Festival.jpg

A week before the procession, always a Sunday, the senior Eyo group, the Adimu with black broad brimmed hats come out in public which signifies that the festival will hold on the following Saturday. Each of the four other important groups: Laba (red hat), Oniko (yellow hat) Ologede (green hat), and Agere (purple hat) take their turn in that order from Monday to Thursday.

On Eyo day, the procession moves from Idumota and through Lagos Island, various locations and landmarks in the city to the king’s palace (Iga Iduganran) where they pay homage to the reigning king. The main highway from the end of Carter Bridge to Tinubu square is closed to vehicles, motorcycles and bicycles. During the festival footwear, head gear of any sort (for both men and women) and “suku”- a local woven hairstyle for women- is prohibited. Violators are beaten by the Eyo masquerade with the staff they carry.
The first Eyo procession was held on the 20th February, 1854 to commemorate the life of Oba Akitoye
